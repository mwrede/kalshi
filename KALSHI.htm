A Revolution in Investing

The first stock markets in the 1600s emerged so that Dutch traders (the literal traders of goods) could raise funds to finance their voyages across the world, and in turn investors were promised a share of those returns. Today, that basic principle of investing has not changed much, and we all know of the fortunes that have been won and lost in this world of creating and investing in companies. Think Warren Buffett, Jeff Bezos, Elon Musk who all owe their wealth to the rise of their stocks (Berkshire Hathaway, Amazon, and Tesla respectively). The connector of these investors across the world are exchanges like NYSE, NASDAQ and others that process millions of trades a day, and provide liquidity, and stable pricing.

Kalshi is a new kind of exchange that operates beyond this traditional world of stocks and bonds trading, and introduces trading based on the outcome of events with a simple yes-or-no question. Imagine if those same Dutch investors who put money into a voyage in the 1600s, could hedge their bet by buying No contracts on the question of Will a ship would come back from the voyage?. If a ship didnt come back, rather than losing all their money (which unfortunately happened quite often in the 1600s), these investors would not be totally broke and could recoup some of their losses via their contract.

 

 

 

 


Events Driven Trading

Event trading is not necessarily something novel, because events have always driven financial markets, for example, when government reports come out about a slowing economy, the market responds by selling stocks of companies that may suffer a recession and buying those that may do well. Trading the outcome of events though is something remarkably novel, and it eliminates the need to decipher the impact of events, and lets you bet on the event itself.

How Contracts Are Traded

Now, lets talk about pricing and how these contracts are set up, and youll understand better the incentives in this events-based trading.

Here are a couple basics:

1)    The contract will be priced between 1 and 99 with 99 being most likely and 1 being the least likely

2)    Each contract is worth 1$ if you are right and 0$ if you are wrong

3)    The contracts fluctuate based on the buying and selling of contracts.

4)    You can sell early if you want.

There are two ways to make money on Kalshi:

Holding your contract until the market closes

Selling your position after a favorable fluctuation
